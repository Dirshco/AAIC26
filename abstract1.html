Background

Alzheimer's disease or related dementia (ADRD) diagnoses often involve complex clinical documentation that can overwhelm families.

Large Language Models (LLMs), specific types of Artificial Intelligence (AI), offer a way to simplify medical jargon; however, their accuracy and empathy in a clinical context remain under-evaluated.

Why It Matters

Post-diagnostic materials overwhelm patients and caregivers
Professional clinical text averages a 15.9 grade reading level
AI accuracy and empathy in clinical contexts remain under-evaluated
Risk of "hallucination" in high-stakes health information

Methods

An audit study evaluating 3 LLMs available to the public [Gemini 1.5, GPT-4o, and DeepSeek V3.2] in translating professional post-diagnostic advice sheets into patient-friendly language.

Evaluation Criteria

Clinical Accuracy — fidelity to the original clinical meaning
Readability — Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level formula
Compassion — empathetic, supportive tone of simplified text
All outputs independently rated by three clinicians on a 5-point Likert scale

LLMs: Gemini 1.5 · GPT-4o · DeepSeek V3.2
  • Mean reading grade level decreased from 15.9 to 4.2, a 74% improvement in readability
  • Clinician review demonstrated high utility and fidelity scores (mean 4.5/5.0)
  • Qualitative error analysis: infrequent deficits, primarily within the "Compassion" domain
  • Tone was occasionally perceived as overly clinical or detached

Future Directions

NEAR-TERMEvaluate AI-generated materials directly with patients and caregivers in real-world settings
MID-TERMDevelop strategies to improve empathy and compassionate communication in AI outputs; enhance safeguards to reduce hallucinations and improve reliability
STRATEGICExplore multilingual adaptation and integration of AI tools within Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) clinical workflows

Conclusions

LLMs are great tools that can bridge the health literacy gap by transforming complex clinical data into patient-centered materials.
Integrating AI into post-diagnostic workflows can enhance caregiver support and healthcare equity.
The potential for hallucinations in high-stakes scenarios supports the use of AI as a clinical "co-pilot" within a Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) framework.
